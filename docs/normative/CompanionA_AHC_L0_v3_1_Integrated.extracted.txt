1. Introduction and Governing Principles
1.1 The Problem and the Bounded Claim
The master AHC architecture begins from the claim that a democratic order may preserve aggregate procedural stability while forcing demographic subgraphs below non-compensable viability floors. Companion A specifies the constitutional translation layer through which this architecture becomes publicly contestable, procedurally reviewable, and institutionally incorporable without requiring citizens to perform its full computation.
The claim is bounded. Companion A does not solve legitimacy in general. It does not remove politics. It does not prove that any given polity will adopt AHC. It specifies the constitutional conditions under which a rights-protective cybernetic system may act without demanding blind trust, without centralizing personal dossiers, and without collapsing into either opacity or fully legible gameability.
1.2 Version 3 Additions
Version 3 incorporates findings from a phenomenological simulation — a speculative fiction generated to render the experience of AHC's correct operation from inside a targeted community. The simulation identified five specification gaps and two irreducible limits not found by prior analytical methods. These additions are marked throughout the document with the simulation finding indicator ⊙.

2. The Three Constitutive Tensions
Three tensions bound the Layer 0 problem. They are constitutive — inherent to any counter-majoritarian constitutional architecture — rather than correctable design flaws.
Trust displacement limit: distrust of state-reported social reality cannot be eliminated but only shifted into new institutions, methods, and interfaces whose governability must themselves be justified. AHC makes the trust problem more tractable by distributing it across three independently grounded ATG pillars. It does not resolve the regress.
Observability lag: many harms become reliably measurable only after they have already begun to harden. The leading indicator sublayer reduces this lag. It does not eliminate it. The architecture is structurally retrospective with respect to harms that precede administrative legibility.
Adversarial adaptation arms race: once a measurement architecture becomes known, hostile actors adjust their routing, timing, and concealment strategies. The simulation added a fourth dimension to this tension: adversaries can exploit the interaction between classification tracks, generating acute events as tools for executing slow-decay harms that are simultaneously under review. See Section 10.

3. Reframing the Ashby Problem
Ashby's Law of Requisite Variety requires that a control system possess at least as much complexity as the system it seeks to regulate. The constitutional solution is not reduction of computational complexity to public simplicity, but translation of rights-affecting outputs into semantically contestable form under ratified review structures.


4. Layer 0A and Layer 0B
4.1 The Distinction
Layer 0A is the Public Constitutional Interface: model features, procedures, outputs, and review rights that must remain publicly legible for constitutional legitimacy. Layer 0B is the Adversarial Resilience Boundary: anti-evasion features whose continuous full disclosure is not required for constitutional legitimacy, provided their bounded opacity does not alter the public logic of classification, the rights of challenge, or available remedies.

5.1 Core Definitions — Unchanged

5.2 Three New Mandatory PLOL Fields ⊙
The simulation identified three gaps in the current PLOL specification. All three produce situations where the document is technically compliant but practically insufficient. Three new mandatory fields address them. These are additions to the Semantic Contestability Record specification in Appendix B.


Field A — Uncertainty Statement (Plain Language)
The uncertainty budget currently appears only in the Technical Reproducibility Record. The simulation showed that a community reading their PLOL has no way to know how much of what they are experiencing is visible to the system. A 0.61 soft-harm capture probability — a 39% miss rate on informal harms — is not only an expert-auditing concern. It is the community's right to know.

Field B — Immediate Limitation Disclosure
The simulation identified that communities interpret a Tier 1 classification as providing more immediate protection than it does. A classification that correctly observes harm without preventing it in the immediate term must say so plainly. Anything less is a misrepresentation by omission.

Field C — Resource Access Field
The falsification condition currently requires an independent qualified reviewer — a resource the affected community does not have access to through the capital routing the Tier 1 classification provides. The right and the resource are structurally decoupled. The PLOL must bridge this gap.

7. Anti-Panopticon Sensing Architecture
7.1 Zero-Knowledge Telemetry
Zero-Knowledge Telemetry (ZKT) is a sensing architecture in which the central manifold receives only privacy-preserving aggregates, derivatives, or inferential claims necessary for viability classification, together with cryptographic proofs sufficient to verify their authorized derivation, while being denied direct access to central personally identifying dossiers wherever feasible.


Open Problems 7.A-7.C (cross-domain joint inference without central microdata pooling; subgraph membership validation without identity exposure; privacy budgets preserving sensitivity) remain open and are addressed in Companion D [AHC-IMPL]. The interim specification for Phase 1 Diagnostic Mode uses Secure Aggregation with Differential Privacy noise addition at the node level [Bonawitz et al. 2017], with explicit ε budget declarations in the Technical Reproducibility Record. This is an interim standard, not the full ZKT specification.
7.2 Interim Phase 1 Privacy Standard
During Phase 1 Diagnostic Mode, before full ZKT specification is available, the following minimum requirements apply:
Secure Aggregation protocol: cross-domain compound vulnerability derivatives computed via Bonawitz et al. [2017] Secure Aggregation, preventing individual record exposure during aggregation

Confidence intervals propagated: resulting confidence intervals on φ_{kl}(t) propagated through to classification uncertainty declarations in the PLOL Technical Reproducibility Record
Individual non-identifiability: central manifold receives no data that permits identification of individuals whose contributions constitute the aggregate; verified by independent privacy audit at least annually

8. Sentinel Node Architecture
8.1 Institutional Participation
A sentinel node is an institution or organized civic infrastructure that contributes bounded, rights-relevant, privacy-preserving signals into the AHC sensing network under an authorized participation protocol. Participation requires governance procedures appropriate to the institution's sectoral obligations, including where relevant: data-use authorization, IRB-compatible review, board approval, participant disclosure, or federated member consent.
8.2 Expanded Node Ecology
Version 2 drew the sentinel node ecology primarily from legal and medical institutions. The simulation and prior red team passes identified this as a vulnerability: narrative risk ('secret clinic surveillance network'), decapitation risk, and epistemic skew toward harms legible to those institutions. Version 3 specifies a broader ecology:


Node ecology diversification is required, not optional. No more than 40% of active sentinel nodes in any jurisdiction may come from a single institution type. This prevents both decapitation risk and epistemic monoculture.
8.3 Sentinel Node Mutual Defense Protocol
When a sentinel node faces legal action, regulatory investigation, or coordinated discreditation campaign, the network's institutions have pre-committed obligations: (a) amicus or public support filings where legally appropriate; (b) resource sharing for legal defense costs up to a specified cap; (c) coordinated public statements affirming the node's participation status and methodology compliance. The credibility of the mutual defense commitment deters attacks on individual members.
8.4 Absence-of-Signal Detection ⊙
The simulation suggested an infiltration variant not previously specified: a hostile or captured node that contributes no false signals but simply omits signals about certain harm types. This appears in the independence audit as low signal frequency from an apparently low-harm area, not as correlated-with-state output.


9. Friction-Based Subgraph Nomination and Standing Doctrine
Friction-Based Subgraph Nomination is the process by which recurrent, correlated, multidomain patterns of deterioration generate candidate cohorts for analytic visibility independent of rigid state-defined categories. Three cohort types with distinct standing are specified, plus the Overflow doctrine for cohorts that cannot be fully mapped onto existing legal categories.


Ratification franchise: the franchise for signal-to-ratified transition is limited to affected and relevant parties — affected community representatives, subject-matter experts, and civil society organizations with documented engagement with the cohort's conditions. General provincial or municipal referenda are not appropriate franchise definitions for cohort ratification, as they allow majorities whose interests conflict with the cohort's protection to block ratification of real vulnerability cohorts.

11. Community Standing, Contestation, and Pause Powers
11.1 The Distrust Recognition Principle
Layer 0 must preserve enforceable rights for affected populations regardless of whether those populations trust the architecture. A rights-protective cybernetic architecture that treats distrust as a communication failure rather than a constitutional condition is structurally vulnerable to reproducing the very domination it claims to resist.
11.2 Community Standing
Community standing is the formally recognized authority of affected populations or their accountable representative bodies to challenge methodology, contest local deployment, trigger review, and seek pause or remand under ratified procedures. Community standing is a constitutional requirement of Layer 0, not a communication strategy.
11.3 Pause Powers — Revised Standard
A pause power is a procedurally specified capacity by which affected communities may suspend deployment, expansion, or local incorporation of the architecture pending review of a rights-relevant challenge. Version 3 adds a formal standard for cognizable versus non-cognizable pause grounds, following identification of the dual failure modes of chilling effect (making petitioning socially costly) and petition flooding (overwhelming the system with frivolous petitions).


11.4 Anti-Flooding Mechanism
When petition volume from a single jurisdiction exceeds a calibrated baseline rate, the system flags for coordinated review — distinguishing between genuine community concern generating multiple petitions and coordinated petition flooding. Coordinated flooding patterns route to the Tribunal's Parameter Legitimacy Review channel as evidence of bad-faith engagement, not to the standard pause petition review.
11.5 Pause Power Scope — Jurisdiction-Specific
Pause powers are community-specific in scope. A community's pause power halts deployment in their specific jurisdiction or as applied to their specific cohort. It does not halt deployment for other communities. This prevents the veto-as-fracture attack: using one community's pause power to halt protective measures that benefit other communities, manufacturing inter-community conflict to fracture the political coalition supporting the architecture.
11.6 Community Statistical Support Fund ⊙
The simulation identified that Tier 1 capital routing provides resources for operational costs but not for the expert statistical analysis required to exercise the right to contest methodology. The right and the resource are structurally decoupled. Version 3 adds the Community Statistical Support Fund as a mandatory allocation within Tier 1 capital routing.


12. Governance Franchise Layer
12.1 Privacy-Preserving Civic Franchise Verification
Governance votes over parameter updates are weighted by verified unique personhood rather than token holdings or institutional membership, using privacy-preserving civic franchise verification. The mechanism preserves one-person-one-vote ratification integrity while minimizing Sybil manipulation, privacy exposure, and coercive access distortions.
12.2 Non-Regression Ratchet and Distinction from Recalibration
Any update that reduces the architecture's protective sensitivity requires a higher burden than an update that expands, refines, or clarifies protections (Principle 10.1). Rollback and recalibration are constitutionally distinct acts requiring distinct evidentiary procedures (Proposition 10.1). The full specification of this distinction and the Recalibration Procedure appears in Appendix F.
12.3 Crisis Frequency Cap
Emergency authority for any subgraph is unavailable for renewal when emergency has been active more than T_{cap} days in a rolling 24-month window (specified at 180 days). Continued terminal threshold exceedance after the cap escalates to Structural Review Protocol: mandatory full Layer 0 constitutional review producing either a formal remediation plan, a constitutional amendment process, or an explicit threshold recalibration decision. Emergency authority cannot resume until one of these outputs is produced and enacted.

13. Tribunal and Appeals Architecture
The International Epistemic Tribunal acts as the final appellate body with a strictly epistemic standard of review: states may not defeat a rights-relevant classification merely by appealing to domestic necessity, policy preference, or institutional convenience. They must succeed on one of three distinct review channels.


14. Institutional Birth Roadmap
Three institutional modes of operation — Diagnostic, Domestic Embedding, and Federated Treaty — are dominant modes, not rigid chronological silos. They may overlap, recurse, and partially co-exist. The transition criteria below specify minimum conditions for each mode to be operative, not for the prior mode to cease.


The Complementarity Principle governs Phase 3: federated AHC institutions default toward complementarity with existing treaty bodies (ICCPR, ICESCR, CEDAW, CERD, regional human rights courts) unless replacement or supersession is explicitly justified. Building on existing legitimacy is institutionally more plausible than minting new legitimacy from scratch.

15. Epistemic Validation Protocol
Claims of predictive superiority or rights-detection advantage must be validated through externally legible replication and benchmark comparison. Without this, AHC remains epistemically interesting but constitutionally premature (Proposition 13.1).
The external validator must be an institution with no funding relationship with AHC's development institutions. The benchmark comparator must be specified in advance as the specific aggregate metric AHC claims to outperform, on a pre-specified task with pre-specified success criteria. The minimum case count for predictive superiority claims is three independently documented cases.
The phenomenological simulation is now a permanent component of the epistemic validation protocol. Before any major specification change affecting community-facing interfaces, a simulation calibrated to the new specification is required as part of the pre-release review process.

16. International Embedding and Treaty Complementarity
The Federated Viability Treaty architecture should be designed as complementarity with existing treaty bodies rather than replacement. The FVT's enforcement mechanisms supplement ICCPR Article 2, ICESCR Articles 2-3, CERD's non-discrimination provisions, and regional human rights court jurisdictions. The standing doctrine for AHC-derived evidence before existing treaty bodies is a Phase 2 objective: establishing that AHC viability field outputs meet the evidentiary standards of the Human Rights Committee's General Comment 36 and the Committee on Economic, Social and Cultural Rights' reporting framework.

18. Conclusion
Layer 0 is not the machinery that asks vulnerable populations to trust a mathematically sophisticated system because its designers promise that it is just. It is the machinery that guarantees those populations retain standing, contestation rights, pause powers, review pathways, and intelligible explanation whether or not trust exists.
Version 3 adds five specification improvements arising from a phenomenological simulation that analytical methods did not reach. The compound event protocol, the community contact architecture, the statistical support fund, the revised PLOL fields, and the clarified Layer 0B footer all emerged from rendering the architecture's correct operation from inside a targeted community's experience. The findings confirm that certain classes of architectural vulnerability are only visible from the position of the communities the architecture is designed to protect.
Two new limits are added: the architecture's correctness as its own wall, and the organization prerequisite. Both are named as design truths rather than solvable problems. A framework that pretends otherwise would be less honest than these limits require.
The question the simulation was designed to answer — Will the communities this is designed to protect have the sustained power to win the fights the architecture enables them to start? — was not answered. It was rendered as a condition. That rendering is now in the document's permanent record, which is where it belongs.

19. Minimum Viable Architecture for Phase 1 Deployment
19.1  The Procedural Hypertrophy Problem
The adversarial development of this architecture has followed a predictable pattern: each patch to a detected vulnerability increases procedural complexity, which creates new attack surfaces, which require more patches. There is an inflection point in this process — identified in the ninth adversarial review pass — where the system is technically smarter but practically weaker, because it has become harder to operate consistently than to circumvent strategically.
The response to this pattern is not to stop improving the architecture. It is to distinguish between what must be present in Phase 1 Diagnostic Mode and what improves the system's operation when the institutional infrastructure to support it exists. This distinction defines the Minimum Viable Architecture.
The MVA is not a simplified version of AHC. It is a sequenced deployment of the same architecture, with a clear specification of which elements constitute the irreducible operational core and which elements are protective additions that require Phase 2 or Phase 3 institutional infrastructure to operate without creating new failure modes of their own.
19.2  The Core Function Criterion


Everything else — enforcement mechanisms, compound event protocols, international tribunals, statistical support funds, graduated response tiers — is in service of this criterion. The MVA question is which elements are required for these three conditions to hold versus which elements improve how well the conditions are met.
19.3  The Multi-Audience Constraint
A prior MVA framing assumed the architecture could be defined by selecting a single primary audience and optimizing for that audience's requirements. This framing fails because the mechanisms that could respond to a classification are distributed across multiple audiences simultaneously:


The architecture cannot optimize for one audience without foreclosing others. Simplifying for courts (rigorous methodology, slow documentation) blocks the community pathway. Simplifying for communities (fast, accessible) fails the court pathway. The MVA must maintain live optionality across all four pathways simultaneously. This is a tighter constraint than minimizing the system — it is minimizing the system while preserving multi-audience operability.
The mechanism that makes this possible is the Tripartite Output Sequencing Protocol. A single underlying classification event generates three sequenced outputs calibrated to the timescale and evidentiary standard of each audience. The outputs are cryptographically hash-bridged to a single classification event, preventing semantic drift across registers. This is the MVA's architectural core.

19.4  The Tripartite Output Sequencing Protocol
The current PLOL specification (Appendix B) produces a Semantic Contestability Record and a Technical Reproducibility Record simultaneously. Version 3.1 revises this to a sequenced tripartite structure that cascades outputs by audience timescale. A full revised specification appears in Appendix B of this addition. The protocol summary:


The T+0 output is the most critical. It is generated automatically upon threshold breach with no operator intervention required. The community cannot be held hostage to the litigation-track documentation timeline. If the T+30d technical record is incomplete, delayed, or under revision, the T+0 semantic record remains in force and visible.
The T+72h Civic/Legislative Translation Record is the new middle register identified as missing from prior specifications. It serves the audience that needs more than plain language but less than a full audit trail — NGO directors preparing for city council meetings, organizers filing for injunctions, legislative staff drafting oversight requests. Its specific content requirements and evidentiary standard appear in Appendix I.
19.4.1  Semantic Consistency Across Registers
The three registers use different registers to describe the same underlying classification event. Hostile actors will exploit this multiplicity, characterizing differences in framing as inconsistency in the underlying findings. The hash bridge proves all three outputs derive from the same classification event. But the hash bridge proves origin, not consistency. Each register must therefore include a mandatory Consistency Statement.

B.5  PLOL Failure Conditions (Revised)
PLOL fails constitutional sufficiency if any of the following conditions are present in any register: cannot be understood by its primary audience without expert assistance; omits falsification pathways; materially diverges from linked registers without a Consistency Statement explaining the divergence; omits the uncertainty statement; omits the immediate limitation disclosure; omits the resource access field where the falsification condition requires expert resources; omits the Consistency Statement; or contains a Layer 0B footer that fails the three-component standard.

Appendix I — Policy Attribution Standard
This appendix specifies the evidentiary methodology for the Civic/Legislative Translation Record (T+72h). The record makes policy causal attribution claims designed for civil society advocacy and administrative law challenge. It targets the Administrative Procedure Act (APA) standard — courts may strike down agency actions that are 'arbitrary, capricious, an abuse of discretion, or otherwise not in accordance with law' — rather than the Daubert standard of the T+30d technical record.
The APA standard is the correct vehicle because it does not require proving discriminatory intent (the hardest legal standard) or full scientific reproducibility (the Daubert standard). It requires demonstrating that the agency's stated rationale for a policy is contradicted by the evidence of its outcomes — which is precisely what the AHC viability field computation is designed to produce.

I.2  Administrative Mechanics Mapping
The T+72h record must cite the specific legal instrument that altered the routing conditions for the affected access domain. Routing conditions are altered by: statutes, ordinances, regulations, administrative guidance documents, enforcement priority memoranda, budget allocations, or formal policy changes at any level of government.

I.3  Temporal Granger Linkage
The T+72h record demonstrates that the viability collapse in the affected subgraph followed the policy instrument's effective date. This is not a causal proof — it is a temporal association that, combined with the Administrative Mechanics Mapping, supports the inference of a policy-driven routing change.

◆  The Rational Explanation Failure The APA hook rests on a specific legal vulnerability: when an agency implements a policy with a stated rationale (public safety, fiscal efficiency, administrative simplification), and the viability field computation demonstrates that the policy's documented outcomes are inconsistent with that rationale for a specific demographic subgraph, the agency has failed to provide a rational explanation connecting its stated purpose to its actual effects. This is an APA arbitrary-and-capricious violation independent of whether the agency intended the harm. The T+72h record documents this gap. It does not need to prove the agency acted with discriminatory intent. It needs to show: (1) what the agency said the policy would do; (2) what the viability field shows the policy actually did for the affected subgraph; (3) that the gap between stated and actual outcomes is not accounted for in any publicly available administrative record. This three-part documentation is the APA hook that advocates bring to administrative hearings and courts.

Administrative Mechanics Mapping — Required Elements Full legal citation: Statute, ordinance, or rule identified by name, code section, and effective date. Example: 'City of X Zoning Ordinance 4B (amended [date], effective [date]), codified at [section].' Implementation mechanism: The specific operational pathway by which this instrument altered access conditions for the affected subgraph. Example: 'Section 4B(ii) removes the requirement that developers provide affordable units in rezoned areas, which removed a constraint on rent increases in affected census tracts.' Stated rationale: The agency's documented justification for the instrument, drawn from the administrative record (legislative history, agency comments, public statements). This is what the Rational Explanation Failure will be measured against. Routing table effect: How the instrument changed the constitutional routing table for V_{kl} nodes — what access conditions existed before and after the instrument's effective date.

Unacknowledged confounds are the primary attack surface for hostile respondents.

Rational Explanation Failure  Appendix I.4 — The APA Core The Rational Explanation Failure is documented as a three-part juxtaposition: Stated purpose: Verbatim or close paraphrase of the agency's documented rationale, with administrative record citation. Documented outcome: The viability field finding for the affected subgraph, in plain terms, with hash reference to the T+0 and T+30d records. The gap: An explicit statement that the documented outcome is not explained by the stated purpose, and that the administrative record contains no documentation of how this outcome was anticipated, weighed, or accepted as consistent with the policy's goals. This gap — undocumented in the administrative record — is the APA arbitrary-and-capricious predicate. The record then states: 'Under the APA's arbitrary-and-capricious standard, an agency must examine relevant data and articulate a satisfactory explanation for its action including a rational connection between the facts found and the choice made [Motor Vehicle Manufacturers Ass'n v. This constitutes grounds for an arbitrary-and-capricious challenge.'
